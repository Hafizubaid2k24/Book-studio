The Last Prophet

★★★★☆ (4.5/5)

A detailed book on the life of the Prophet Muhammad (PBUH).

Author: Imam Muhammad

Pages: 500

Level: Intermediate

Go back to books

Book Details

Prophet Muhammad, peace and blessings be upon him, was born in the sacred city of Mecca in 570 CE, an era in Arab history known as the “Year of the Elephant,” a time marked by a miraculous deliverance from destruction. Orphaned at birth—he lost his father, Abdullah, before ever knowing him, and his mother Amina when he was only six—he was raised first by his devoted grandfather, Abdul Muttalib, and later by his compassionate uncle, Abu Talib. Through these formative years, Muhammad earned the reverent nicknames Al-Sadiq, the Truthful, and Al-Amin, the Trustworthy, testament to his impeccable character in a society governed by tribal loyalties and mercantile schemes. In his youth, he worked as a shepherd and then as a merchant, traveling across the Arabian Peninsula and earning a reputation for fairness, kindness, and moral uprightness. At twenty-five, he married Khadijah, a wealthy and honorable widow who recognized in him both integrity and quiet strength. She stood by him through fifteen peaceful and fulfilling years. Yet, beneath daily life, his spirit yearned for deeper meaning. At age forty, during a solitary retreat in the cave of Hira atop Jabal al-Nur, he received history’s most transformative message when the Angel Gabriel appeared with the command: “Read! In the name of your Lord who created.” This divine encounter marked his call to prophethood. At first, Muhammad relayed God’s message quietly to close family and friends, but soon he was urged to share it more broadly. His message—a call to worship one God, uphold justice, and eliminate idolatry—stirred the custodians of Mecca’s idol-worshiping customs. They responded with opposition, persecution, and painful suppression. Loyal companions, many of whom were marginalized in society, faced exile, torture, and heartbreak. Yet the Prophet remained patient, unwavering in faith, and resolute in compassion. Unable to bear the oppression any longer, the Muslim community received divine guidance to migrate to Yathrib—later known as Medina. There, in 622 CE, Muhammad arrived in triumph not as a conqueror, but as a unifying leader, forging bonds between the emigrants (Muhajirun) and the helpers (Ansar), establishing the Prophet’s Mosque, and drafting the Constitution of Medina—a charter of coexistence guaranteeing freedom of faith, social equity, and mutual protection. But peace was fragile; as the early Islamic state matured, threats loomed. Battles such as Badr, Uhud, and the Trench tested the moral and military fabric of this nascent community. The Prophet’s balanced genius—strategic acumen blended with humility and mercy—guided each critical moment, revealing not only a commander’s heart but a shepherd’s soul. Near the eighth Islamic year, his devoted followers numbered in the thousands, and tensions escalated. In a monumental moment of forgiveness, Muhammad peacefully entered Mecca with a great assembly, declaring: “Today there is no blame upon you—go, you are all free.” The idols were cleared from the Kaaba, and its sanctity was fully restored to the worship of One God. This turning point was one of the most profound mercy-charged reconciliations ever witnessed, defined neither by retribution nor resentment, but by grace and dignity. Two years later, in 632 CE, he performed what would become known as the Farewell Pilgrimage. From the sands before Mount Arafat, he delivered a deeply moving sermon that resonates across centuries—a sweeping manifesto of human rights, equality, and spiritual morality. He affirmed that no Arab is superior to a non-Arab and no non-Arab superior to an Arab, save by piety and righteousness. He championed the sanctity of life, property, and honor, and reinforced faith through unwavering adherence to the Qur’an and his teachings. Shortly after returning to Medina, he fell ill. Surrounded by family and companions, at age sixty-three, he passed away—leaving behind no wealth, no palace, no formal dynasty—only the monumental legacy of guidance, mercy, and community. In those final days, his true estate was evident: a bond among believers, a living tradition, and an enduring light. Through adversity and triumph, he remained a devoted servant of God, a just ruler, a caring father, a loyal friend, and a master of spiritual as well as temporal affairs. His story—of an orphan in Mecca evolving into a global beacon—continues to inspire billions. Through his tears and triumphs, wars and reconciliations, he upheld sincerity, humility, and compassion. His message—one God, justice for all, kindness toward the weak, and integrity in action—reverberates as strongly today as it did 14 centuries ago. It stands not only as a part of history but as living guidance for humanity. Whether learned in solitude or celebrated in community, the life of Muhammad ﷺ remains the most resplendent chapter in the story of faith and humanity—an endlessly unfolding chapter of hope, light, and profound awe..

Seerah: The Life of the Prophet

A comprehensive study of the life and teachings of the Prophet.

Author: Ibn Ishaq

Pages: . 768

Book Details

Prophet Muhammad, peace be upon him, was born in the city of Mecca in the year 570 CE, known in Arab history as the “Year of the Elephant.” Orphaned before birth by the death of his father, Abdullah, and losing his mother, Amina, at the tender age of six, he was placed under the care of his grandfather Abdul Muttalib, and later his uncle Abu Talib. From his youth, he was renowned among the Quraysh tribe for his honesty, integrity, and trustworthiness, earning the titles “Al-Sadiq” (the truthful) and “Al-Amin” (the trustworthy). As a young man, Muhammad worked as a shepherd and later as a merchant, traveling across Arabia and beyond, engaging in trade that brought him both material sustenance and a deep understanding of human nature. At the age of twenty-five, he married Khadijah, a wealthy and noble merchant widow, whose respect and affection for him grew from witnessing his impeccable character. For the next fifteen years, he lived a peaceful and respected life in Mecca. However, his heart was restless in the face of the city’s moral corruption, idolatry, and social injustices. Often, he would retreat to the Cave of Hira, on the mountain of light, Jabal al-Nur, for meditation and spiritual reflection. It was during one of these solitary vigils, at the age of forty, in the month of Ramadan, that the angel Gabriel (Jibreel) appeared to him with the first revelation: “Read, in the name of your Lord who created.” This marked the beginning of his prophethood. Initially, the message of Islam—calling people to worship one God alone and to abandon idolatry—was shared in private with close family and friends, but as the revelations continued, he was commanded to proclaim it publicly. The Quraysh leaders, threatened by the loss of their power and traditions, responded with hostility, ridicule, and persecution. His followers, many of whom were poor or socially vulnerable, endured severe torture, exile, and even death. Despite this, Muhammad remained steadfast, embodying patience, courage, and unwavering faith. For over a decade, the persecution in Mecca intensified until the command came to migrate to the city of Yathrib—later named Medina—where he was welcomed as a leader, peacemaker, and judge. In Medina, he established the first Islamic state, built the Prophet’s Mosque, and forged a pact of brotherhood between the emigrants (Muhajirun) and the helpers (Ansar). He drafted the Constitution of Medina, ensuring religious freedom and social justice for Muslims, Jews, and others living in the city. Yet the enemies of Islam did not relent; battles such as Badr, Uhud, and the Trench tested the faith and unity of the Muslim community. Through both victory and hardship, Muhammad’s leadership combined strategic brilliance with mercy and humility. In the eighth year after the migration, following years of tension and broken treaties, he marched to Mecca with a vast army. The city, expecting vengeance, instead received forgiveness: “No blame will there be upon you today; you are all free,” he declared, erasing the bitterness of years with one stroke of mercy. The idols in the Kaaba were removed, and the sanctuary was restored to the pure worship of God. Two years later, in the tenth year of Hijra, Muhammad performed his Farewell Pilgrimage, delivering a sermon that remains one of the most profound declarations of human rights, equality, and moral duty in history. He reminded the believers that no Arab has superiority over a non-Arab, nor a non-Arab over an Arab, except by piety and righteousness. He emphasized the sanctity of life, property, and honor, and urged adherence to the Qur’an and his Sunnah as the guiding lights for all time. Shortly after returning to Medina, he fell ill. Surrounded by his family and companions, he passed away at the age of sixty-three, leaving behind no wealth or property, but a legacy that would transform the world. His life, from the orphaned child of Mecca to the leader of a community founded on justice, compassion, and faith, continues to inspire over a billion people today. He was not only a prophet but also a husband, father, friend, statesman, and servant of God, whose example remains the highest moral standard for humankind. His message—of worshipping one God, practicing mercy, upholding justice, and caring for the weak—resonates as powerfully now as it did fourteen centuries ago, offering timeless guidance to any soul seeking truth and peace..

Saints and Heroes

★★★★☆ (4.6/5)

Stories of great Islamic figures and their achievements.

Author:James Becket and Mosab Hassan Yousef

Pages: 368

Level: Elementary

Book Details

In the mosaic of human history, there shines a constellation of extraordinary souls—saints and heroes whose lives transcend ordinary bounds, stirring our hearts with stories of courage, compassion, and indomitable spirit. Among them emerges Saint Juan Diego, the humble Nahua peasant who, in 1531, encountered the Virgin Mary on Tepeyac Hill and carried her miraculous image to the world—a testament to profound faith blooming in the simplest heart. Centuries later, Saint Teresa of Ávila, cloaked in the swirling mists of 16th-century Spain, reformed the Carmelite order with unyielding resolve, her fervent prayers igniting spiritual revolutions and shaping mystical contemplations for generations to come. We travel further through time to stand beside Saint Francis de Sales, speaking with gentle eloquence into hearts of doubt and despair, his warm voice offering solace and hope—teaching that holiness is not an unreachable summit, but a tender path walked in everyday love. And who could forget Philip Neri, whose joyful laughter echoed through the streets of Rome, reminding a weary world that sanctity resides as much in levity as in devotion, inviting the faithful to dance with grace toward God? Childhood’s whispers conjure images of Blessed Teresa of Calcutta, barefoot and serene, treading the fevered alleys of Calcutta, cradling broken lives in her arms, weaving threads of mercy where despair had woven grief. Alongside her stands Jean Vanier, whose gentle hands built homes for people cast aside by society, recognizing in each fragile soul the spark of divine dignity, teaching us that true heroism breathes in compassion, not conquest. Yet, these modern beacons find ancient company in legends etched by faith’s imagination—Saint Jerome, his loyal companion the gentle lion, a bond forged not by blood but by mutual trust, teaching that kindness tames even the fiercest hearts; Saint Brendan the Sailor, braving ocean’s mysteries on a tiny boat, chasing the horizon toward unseen lands and truths, urging us to voyage beyond comfort to discover the divine in the unknown. In each account, the author unfolds their humanity: the beggar turned king, the poet turned preacher, the fool turned sage—souls deemed insignificant by society yet chosen to reflect divine light. Their stories span continents and centuries, from the jungles of Mexico to the cobbled lanes of medieval Europe, weaving a tapestry where every thread glimmers with challenge and redemption. In 33 lively chapters, these lives spring forth—not as distant relics but as breathing companions, daring us to confront injustice, to extend mercy, to pursue truth. We meet Saint Elizabeth of Hungary, weaving miracles from her royal duties, offering bread to the poor until her very cloak transformed into roses when challenged by her husband—proof that faith can bloom amid privilege. We linger with Saint Vincent de Paul, whose burning love for the poor transformed charities and institutions, his legacy echoing in every hospital ward and orphanage that still bears his name. And beyond chronicles of nobility, the author honors unexpected heroes—the “cranks,” the “fools,” the overlooked—whose eccentricities conceal divine purpose. In their quirks reside courage; in their oddities, the power to challenge the world’s expectations. With illustrative head drawings that grace each chapter, their portraits invite us to see not haloed perfection, but flawed, beloved humanity—inviting us to imagine ourselves in their stories, beckoning the spark of heroism within our own hearts. This anthology, bridging legendary myth and historical fact, invites readers—especially young adults—to step into a realm where faith and legend entwine, where saints stand not on distant pedestals, but beside us, whispering: “You too can rise.” It asserts that holiness emerges not from isolation, but through engagement with suffering, society, the marginalized, the joyous, the everyday human. These saints and heroes are companions through time—soldiers and sailors, kings and queens, doctors and beggars, poets and diplomats—each offering a mirror to our own potential for courage, for generosity, for longing that reaches beyond self. In these pages unfolds a grand invitation: to look at history not as a silent archive, but as a living chorus of voices that refuse to be muted—the saint whose prayer broke chains, the hero whose mercy changed hearts, the unknown whose kindness altered destiny. They stand as “light-bearers,” luminous examples that enthral future generations, not through polished perfection but through daring humility, radical love, and the courage to answer a call greater than self. Ultimately, this anthology is more than a collection—it is a journey through the heart of humanity and sanctity, urging each of us to believe that even the smallest spark can become a beacon—and that Saints and Heroes are not merely those remembered in pages, but those whose stories continue to shape us, beckoning us to rise, to serve, to shine.

The Great Caliphs

★★★★☆ (4.7/5)

Author: Amira K. Bennison

Pages:256

Level: Intermediate

Book Details

This book provides a detailed discussion of the great sacrifices and principles of governance of Hazrat Abu Bakr, Hazrat Umar, and Hazrat Ali (R.A).Amira K. Bennison's "The Great Caliphs" offers a re-evaluation of the 'Abbasid caliphate (750-1258 CE), a period often considered the classical age of Islamic civilization. The central thesis of the book is to challenge the popular misconception that Islamic civilization was a monolithic, alien culture that arose in opposition to the Western Graeco-Roman world. Instead, Bennison argues that the 'Abbasid Empire was a natural successor to the great civilizations of antiquity, including those of Greece, Rome, Byzantium, and Persia. By placing the 'Abbasid era within this broader historical context, the book highlights the continuity of cultural and intellectual traditions across the Mediterranean and Near East. Bennison posits that the Arabs, far from attempting to purge pre-existing cultures, were active participants in a process of cultural synthesis, preserving, systematizing, and building upon the knowledge they inherited from older civilizations. This approach debunks the idea of a sharp cultural divide between East and West, demonstrating how the 'Abbasids were inheritors of "civilized" values that Western civilization often claims as its own unique legacy. The book delves into various facets of 'Abbasid society to support this argument. It begins with a political framework, tracing the rise of the 'Abbasids and the major developments that shaped their empire, from the move of the capital to Baghdad to the challenges posed by internal conflicts and external pressures like the Crusades. Bennison then explores the thriving urban life of the period, showing how 'Abbasid cities were not only centers of political power but also hubs of commerce and intellectual exchange. She documents how urban planning and architectural styles were influenced by earlier models while also developing distinctively Islamic features. A significant portion of the book is dedicated to the intellectual "Golden Age" of Baghdad, particularly the famed translation movement. Here, Bennison details the systematic effort by Muslim scholars to translate and study works of Greek and Persian philosophy, medicine, mathematics, and astronomy. She emphasizes that this was not merely a process of transmission; 'Abbasid scholars made their own significant contributions, advancing these fields in ways that would later prove invaluable to European thinkers during the Renaissance. The book thus portrays the 'Abbasid Empire not as an isolated cultural entity but as a dynamic, cosmopolitan civilization that played a crucial role in the global history of ideas.
